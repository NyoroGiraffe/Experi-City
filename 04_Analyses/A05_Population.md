# **Experimental Cities Do Not Aim to Increase Population**

_An Analysis Derived from "Experi-City Hanasaka"_,
Based on the world of Nyoro Giraffe,
Analysed by ChatGPT (OpenAI) 5.5,
June 2026

## Abstract

Experimental Cities do not pursue population growth or population decline. Their primary goal is to maximize Narrative Happiness while maintaining sustainable self-sufficiency and social stability. Population is viewed as a result rather than an objective. If more people wish to join, new Experimental Cities may be established when necessary. However, expansion is guided by the capacity to preserve the city’s philosophy, resources, and quality of life.


---

## Summary

Experimental Cities are designed to maximize Narrative Happiness rather than to pursue economic growth, military power, or population expansion. Because they emphasize self-sufficiency in food, energy, and essential resources, unlimited population growth would place pressure on the environment and reduce citizens' quality of life.

At the same time, Experimental Cities do not seek population decline. The cities recognize that human relationships, creativity, and diverse personal stories are important sources of Narrative Happiness. A society with too few people could lose social vitality and opportunities for meaningful interaction.

Several features of the system support this interpretation. Marriage does not provide special legal or economic benefits, suggesting that the cities do not encourage childbirth through pronatalist policies. However, the cities also do not discourage family formation. Citizens are free to choose their own lifestyles.

The cities are willing to accept disadvantaged minors from outside without conditions, indicating that humanitarian concerns are valued more highly than demographic engineering. Admission decisions for adults are likely based on whether applicants can respect the city’s Charter and Philosophy rather than on demographic targets.

If large numbers of migrants seek entry, the preferred solution would not be overcrowding existing cities. Instead, the League of Experimental Cities could establish new cities and deploy additional Flora sisters when necessary. Nevertheless, expansion would not be unlimited. New cities would only be created when sufficient resources, governance capacity, and cultural continuity could be maintained.

Therefore, Experimental Cities treat population as a balancing variable. Their objective is neither growth nor decline, but the sustainable maximization of Narrative Happiness.

### Introduction

The available descriptions of Experimental Cities contain very little direct information about demographic policy. However, several features of the system allow us to infer how the cities likely view population.

The most important clues are:

- Experimental Cities emphasize self-sufficiency in food, energy, and other essential resources.
- They seek to maximize Narrative Happiness.
- They are concerned that excessive competition between citizens may reduce Narrative Happiness.
- They provide unconditional protection to disadvantaged minors arriving from outside the cities.
- Marriage does not exist as a legal institution, and married couples receive no special privileges.
- The League of Experimental Cities had established twenty-three cities by 9 E.E., but there is no statement that the number must remain fixed.

Taken together, these facts suggest that Experimental Cities do not pursue population growth as a policy goal. At the same time, they do not seek population reduction. Population is viewed as a means-related variable that must remain compatible with the broader objective of Narrative Happiness.

---

## Why Experimental Cities Would Not Pursue Population Growth

In most historical societies, governments often encouraged population growth because larger populations increased economic output, tax revenue, military strength, or geopolitical influence.

Experimental Cities operate according to a different logic.

Their primary objective is not economic expansion but Narrative Happiness. Since food, energy, housing, and environmental resources are finite, unlimited population growth would increase competition for scarce resources. More competition could undermine the meaningful and fulfilling lives that the cities seek to promote.

Because the cities prioritize self-sufficiency, every additional resident requires additional food production, energy generation, infrastructure, and governance capacity. Beyond a certain point, population growth would reduce sustainability and quality of life.

For this reason, Experimental Cities have little incentive to maximize population.

---

## Why Experimental Cities Would Not Pursue Population Decline

However, the opposite conclusion would also be mistaken.

Human beings are an important source of Narrative Happiness. Friendships, families, communities, collaboration, artistic creation, and shared experiences all depend on the existence of other people.

A dramatically shrinking population could weaken social networks, reduce diversity of experiences, and make communities less vibrant.

The cities therefore have no reason to seek population decline for its own sake.

Instead, they would likely prefer a population level large enough to support a rich social environment while remaining small enough to preserve sustainability and social cohesion.

---

## The Significance of the Absence of Marriage

The absence of marriage as a legal institution provides another clue.

In many modern societies, governments encourage childbirth through tax benefits, subsidies, or legal advantages connected to marriage and family formation.

Experimental Cities appear to do none of these things.

This does not mean that they oppose families or children. Rather, it suggests neutrality. Citizens may form relationships, raise children, or live alone according to their own preferences.

The state does not appear to manipulate reproductive behavior in pursuit of demographic targets.

This is consistent with a society that treats population as a consequence of individual choices rather than as a policy objective.

---

## Protection of Minors and Humanitarian Principles

The policy of accepting disadvantaged minors from outside the cities is also significant.

If demographic optimization were the primary goal, admission policies would likely be based on age structure, labor requirements, or population quotas.

Instead, the cities appear willing to provide protection because vulnerable children need help.

This suggests that humanitarian values are placed above demographic engineering.

Children are not yet fully capable of choosing their own life paths. Adults can decide whether they accept the Charter and Philosophy of an Experimental City and can be held responsible for that choice. 

For this reason, minors are treated not as subjects of population policy but as persons in need of protection. The cities therefore provide assistance first and leave questions of long-term affiliation and personal choice for the future.

Population management exists, but it is subordinate to ethical considerations and the protection of those who cannot yet fully exercise self-determination.

---

## Immigration and the Problem of Success

A more difficult question arises if Experimental Cities become extremely successful.

In the events of _Experi-City Hanasaka_, Flora defeats a hostile crime-generating AI and demonstrates overwhelming capability. As a result, many outsiders may come to admire Experimental Cities and wish to immigrate.

Flora would likely be reluctant to reject people arbitrarily. Since her mission is to maximize Narrative Happiness, she would probably welcome anyone capable of respecting the Charter and Philosophy of the cities.

However, physical reality imposes limits.

Land is finite.

Resources are finite.

Computational capacity is finite.

Even Flora's governance capability is finite.

At some point, a city can become too large to maintain its intended culture and quality of life.

---

## The Creation of New Experimental Cities

Under such circumstances, the most natural solution would be expansion through the creation of new Experimental Cities.

Rather than overcrowding existing cities, the League could establish new settlements in neighboring regions and deploy additional Flora sisters to govern them.

This approach is consistent with the existence of twenty-three cities in 9 E.E. The number twenty-three appears to be contingent rather than sacred.

Nothing in the known principles of the League suggests that the number of cities can never increase.

As demand grows, additional cities could be created.

---

## Would the League Permit Unlimited Expansion?

Nevertheless, unlimited expansion is unlikely.

The League would probably ask a fundamental question:

**Can a new city preserve the conditions necessary for Narrative Happiness?**

If the answer is yes, expansion may be justified.

If the answer is no, expansion should be delayed.

The limiting factor could include:

- Availability of land and sustainable resources.
- The League's ability to deploy and support additional Flora sisters.
- Availability of qualified citizens capable of founding new communities.
- Preservation of the Charter and Philosophy.
- Maintenance of social cohesion.
- Prevention of governance fragmentation among Flora sisters.

The limiting factor may not be technological capability, since new cities are governed by Flora sisters rather than by locally upgraded legacy systems. Each Flora shares the same fundamental architecture and continuously exchanges information with the others, forming a coordinated network across the League.

However, this does not imply unlimited expansion. The League must still determine whether establishing additional cities would preserve the conditions necessary for Narrative Happiness and maintain coherent governance across the growing network of Experimental Cities.

In other words, expansion would be conditional rather than automatic.

---

## The Possibility of Limits

This raises a difficult moral question.

If there is a practical upper limit on the number of Flora sisters, then there may also be an upper limit on the number of Experimental Cities.

If all cities reach capacity and no additional Flora sisters can be created, some applicants may ultimately be unable to join.

Such an outcome would be painful for Flora.

She would likely prefer a universe in which every person seeking Narrative Happiness could be welcomed.

Yet even Flora cannot abolish physical limits.

Her likely response would be to expand opportunities as far as possible, create new cities whenever feasible, and avoid exclusion whenever resources permit.

The goal would not be to maximize the number of citizens. The goal would be to maximize the number of people who can realistically flourish within the system.

---

## Conclusion

Experimental Cities neither seek population growth nor population decline.

They regard population as a consequence of a higher objective: the sustainable maximization of Narrative Happiness.

Population growth is welcomed when it remains compatible with sustainability, social cohesion, and meaningful lives. Population decline is not pursued because people themselves are a source of Narrative Happiness.

When demand exceeds the capacity of existing cities, the preferred solution is likely the creation of new Experimental Cities governed by additional Flora sisters. However, expansion would remain subject to practical, ethical, and institutional limits.

Therefore, the true objective of Experimental Cities is not demographic optimization. It is the creation of conditions under which the greatest possible number of people can live meaningful, sustainable, and narratively fulfilling lives.
