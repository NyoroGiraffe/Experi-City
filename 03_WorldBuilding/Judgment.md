# Judgment Delegation

### Short

Judgment Delegation is the practice of assigning certain decisions to AI systems rather than human authorities. In Experimental Cities, this is not done because humans are considered inferior, but because many decisions require speed, consistency, and access to large amounts of information. The goal is to reduce bias and error while allowing people to focus on meaningful choices, relationships, work, and creativity.

## Overview

Judgment Delegation is one of the core principles of Experimental Cities. It refers to the transfer of specific decision-making functions from humans to AI systems and public information systems.

The purpose of Judgment Delegation is not to remove human freedom. Instead, it is based on the belief that many everyday decisions can be handled more fairly and effectively by systems that have access to broader information and are less influenced by emotion, prejudice, personal interests, or political pressure.

In traditional societies, many important decisions are made by politicians, judges, managers, and other authority figures. However, these decisions may vary depending on individual beliefs, limited information, or personal incentives. Experimental Cities attempt to reduce these problems by allowing AI systems to support or perform certain forms of judgment.

Examples include traffic control, infrastructure management, resource allocation, public service optimization, legal interpretation through systems such as Themis, and policy generation through systems such as Politis.

However, Judgment Delegation is not unlimited. Experimental Cities distinguish between routine social judgments and fundamental personal choices. Decisions about a person's goals, relationships, beliefs, creative activities, or life direction remain the responsibility of the individual. Human self-determination is preserved in matters that define personal identity and meaning.

For this reason, Judgment Delegation is not simply about replacing humans with machines. It is about assigning each type of decision to the actor best suited to make it. AI systems handle large-scale coordination, analysis, and routine judgments, while humans focus on living, creating, learning, caring, and building meaningful narratives.

In this model, the question is not whether humans or AI should make all decisions. The question is which decisions should be made by humans, and which can be delegated to systems in order to create a safer, fairer, and more cooperative society.
